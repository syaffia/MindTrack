Introduction

MindTrack is a mobile application designed to support mental health and wellbeing monitoring among university students. The application provides a simple and interactive platform that allows users to reflect on their emotional state, understand stress levels, and access supportive mental health resources.

The system encourages users to monitor their daily emotional condition through a Mood Check-In feature, where users can select their current mood using an interactive mood interface. These mood records are then used to generate Mood Insights, which help users understand their emotional trends over time.

MindTrack also includes a Burnout Detection module that analyzes users’ emotional patterns and estimates their stress level. When potential signs of burnout are detected, the system provides helpful insights and encourages users to take preventive actions.

To further support mental wellbeing, the application provides educational articles related to stress management, mental health awareness, and healthy lifestyle practices. In addition, MindTrack integrates an AI chatbot called Doctor Capy AI, which allows users to communicate their feelings and receive supportive guidance or recommendations for professional help.

The application is designed using user-friendly interfaces and engaging visual elements to make mental health monitoring simple, approachable, and accessible for students.

Problem Statement

Mental health issues such as stress, anxiety, and burnout are increasingly common among university students. Academic workloads, social pressures, and lifestyle changes often contribute to emotional exhaustion and reduced wellbeing.

Many students experience stress but do not regularly monitor their emotional condition. As a result, early signs of burnout or emotional distress may go unnoticed until the situation becomes more serious.

Although several mental health applications exist, many of them focus only on meditation or basic mood logging and may not provide clear insights into users’ emotional patterns. Some applications also have complicated interfaces that discourage regular use.

Therefore, there is a need for a simple and engaging mental wellbeing monitoring application that helps students track their mood, understand stress levels, and access helpful mental health resources in an easy and supportive way.

Literature Review

Previous studies have shown that digital mental health tools can help individuals improve emotional awareness and manage stress more effectively. Mobile applications provide convenient platforms for users to monitor their mental wellbeing through regular self-reflection and emotional tracking.

Research in Human Computer Interaction (HCI) highlights the importance of usability and user-centered design in health-related applications. Applications designed with simple interfaces, clear navigation, and supportive feedback can improve user engagement and encourage continuous use.

Mood tracking features are commonly used in mental health applications to help users reflect on their emotions and identify patterns in their daily experiences. Visualizing mood data through charts or statistics allows users to better understand changes in their emotional state over time.

In addition, conversational technologies such as AI chatbots have been increasingly used in mental health support systems. These chatbots can provide immediate responses, emotional support, and guidance when users experience stress or emotional difficulties.

Proposed System

MindTrack is designed as a mobile application that helps users monitor their emotional wellbeing through mood tracking, burnout detection, and mental health resources.

The application begins with a Welcome screen that introduces the purpose of the system. Users can create an account through the Sign Up page or access their account through the Log In page.

After logging in, users are directed to the Home page, which acts as the main dashboard of the application. The home screen provides quick access to the main features such as Mood Check-In, Burnout Detection, and mental health articles.

The Mood Check-In feature allows users to record their daily emotional state by selecting their mood from an interactive mood selection interface. This feature encourages users to reflect on how they feel and maintain awareness of their emotional condition.

The Burnout Detection module analyzes mood records to estimate the user’s stress level. The system provides a stress score and identifies possible stress indicators to help users understand potential burnout risks.

The application also provides an Articles section, where users can read informative content related to mental health topics such as stress management, meditation, and emotional wellbeing. Users can search articles and explore suggested topics to improve their understanding of mental health.

MindTrack also includes an AI Chatbot feature called Doctor Capy AI, which allows users to communicate their emotional concerns. The chatbot provides supportive responses and may recommend professional therapists when signs of emotional distress are detected.

The system also generates Mood Insights, which present weekly mood statistics and visual charts to help users understand emotional trends and reflect on their wellbeing.

Finally, users can access their Profile and Settings page to manage personal information, adjust notification settings, enable dark mode, and access help center resources.

Design Principles Applied

The design of MindTrack follows one of Human Computer Interaction principles to ensure usability and positive user experience.

Feedback

One of the main Human Computer Interaction (HCI) design principles applied in MindTrack is Feedback. Feedback refers to the system’s ability to provide clear and immediate responses to users after they perform an action. Effective feedback helps users understand what is happening in the system and confirms that their actions have been successfully recognized.

In the MindTrack application, feedback is implemented through several features that visually and interactively respond to user actions.

For example, in the Mood Check-In feature, when users select their emotional state, the system immediately displays the selected mood such as “I feel Neutral” along with visual cues and animations. This response confirms that the user’s input has been successfully recorded.

Another example can be seen in the Burnout Detection feature. After analyzing the user's emotional data, the system provides a clear stress level score and displays indicators such as “Moderately Stressed Out.” This feedback helps users understand their current mental state and encourages them to reflect on their wellbeing.

The AI Chatbot (Doctor Capy AI) also demonstrates feedback by responding to user messages in real time. When a user expresses feelings of burnout or stress, the chatbot provides supportive messages and may recommend professional therapists. This interaction reassures users that their concerns are acknowledged and that help is available.

In addition, the Mood Insights page provides visual feedback through weekly mood statistics and charts. These visualizations help users understand emotional patterns over time and provide meaningful information about their mental health trends.

By applying the feedback design principle, MindTrack ensures that users receive immediate and meaningful responses to their interactions. This improves user understanding, enhances engagement, and creates a more supportive and responsive mental health monitoring experience.

Storyboard

The storyboard illustrates how users interact with the MindTrack application during their daily routine.

1. The user opens the MindTrack application in the morning.

2. The system prompts the user to log their mood and daily goals.

3. Throughout the day, the system monitors behavioral patterns such as phone usage and activity levels.

4. If stress patterns are detected, the system sends a notification suggesting relaxation activities.

Evaluation Plan

Usability Testing

To evaluate the usability of the system, testing will be conducted with 5–10 participants who are university students.

  • Task 1: Log daily mood
  • Task 2: Write a journal entry
  • Task 3: Review the wellbeing dashboard
  • Task 4: View weekly reports

Metrics such as task completion time, user satisfaction, and error frequency will be collected to measure system usability.

  • 1. Was the app easy to understand when you first used it?
  • 2. How easy was it to navigate between different pages of the app?
  • 3. Did you feel comfortable using the interface?
  • 4. Did you encounter any difficulties while using the app?
  • 5. How satisfied are you with the overall design of the app?
  • 6. Was it easy to find the mood logging feature?
  • 7. How easy was it to record your daily mood?
  • 8. Was the journal writing feature easy to use?
  • 9. Did the interface make it comfortable for you to write your thoughts?
  • 10. Was the wellbeing dashboard easy to understand?
  • 11. Did the dashboard clearly show your mood or wellbeing information?
  • 12. Was it easy to access the weekly report section?
  • 13. Did the weekly report help you understand your mood trends?
  • 14. How satisfied are you with the speed of completing tasks in the app?
  • 15. Would you use this app regularly for tracking your wellbeing?
  • 16. What did you like most about the app?
  • 17. What improvements would you suggest for the app?

Ethical Considerations

  • User data is encrypted and securely stored.
  • Users must provide consent before data collection.
  • Users can control privacy settings and disable sensors.
  • The system does not share personal data with third parties.
  • Users can permanently delete their account.

Conclusion

MindTrack shows how modern mobile technology and user-centered design can be combined to create a meaningful digital wellbeing companion. Through continuous mood sensing, emotional insights, and accessible support features, the application empowers students to better understand their mental health and take preventive actions before burnout becomes severe.
